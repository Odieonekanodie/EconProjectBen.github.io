Ukraine, Russia, and how it Relates to Raid and Trade

Ben Renegar, Public Economics, 2024, First Website

Introduction

Argument:

Through raiding, Russia hoped to gain better trade.

Russia initially invaded Ukraine in 2014 to secure the port of Sevastapol. The combination of a lack of meaningful international condemnation and the overall hostility that the Ukrainian government had towards Russia only encouraged the Russian government to launch a full scale invasion in 2022.

This was likely due to the desire to replace the current Ukrainian government with a friendly one, which would allow Russia to regain the influence it lost in the country back in 2014. Hence the header.

Part 1: The Prelude

Break up of the USSR

  • The USSR gradually broke up, finally being dissolved in 1991.
  • Split into 16 states (if Chechnya is included).
  • These states struggled economically, especially with shock policy[1][2].
  • Ukraine and Russia were no exception.

As shown, both economies experienced slumps after the fall of the USSR.

Russia's Influence

  • Russia was the most powerful post soviet state.
  • Many of the post soviet countries remained under its influence as a result.
  • The CSTO is an example of this.
  • Ukraine was also under this influence.
  • Ukraine would sign the Kharkiv Pact with Russia in 1997.
  • This gave Russia access to the Sevastapol Port in Crimea.
  • Sevastapol is strategically important, due to ease of access to the Black Sea.

Part 2: Hard Power Versus Soft Power

There are two primary ways to describe a country's influence: Hard Power and Soft Power.

  • Hard Power: The usage of coersion to influence others. This is primarily done through the use of military and economic means.

    • Military alliances, like the CSTO, are an example of Hard Power. Not only do military alliances put greater emphasis on its stronger members, but they are also often economically linked with each other. While the CSTO has weakened over the years, the member states are still very dependent on Russia economically and militarily[3][4].

  • Soft Power: Non coersive influence of others. This can be done through ideology, culture, and more.

    • Russia has a sizeable amount of soft power, particularly through its influence of other countries' politics. As for Ukraine, one big example of the usage of soft power is the promotion of the Russian Orthodox Church, which is under the Patriarch of Moscow, Kirill, who is a close ally of Putin[5].

    A demonstration of Russian hard power.

    Part 3: The West

    Lets take a moment and examine the West, more specifically the EU.

    • Russia has long been supplying the EU with its energy needs.
    • This was particularly the case with natural gas.
    • Russian supply of natural gas to Europe saw an overall gradual increase.
    • Supplied 32% of natual gas to the EU as of 2021[6][7][8].

    Economic Interdependence

    Supply of natural gas and various other resources by Russia to the European Union, as well as European supply of goods to Russia, which will not be focused on here, created a large amount of economic interdependence between Russia and the EU.

    • Helped increase Russia's influence in the region.
    • Reliance on gas made it more difficult for dependent countries to go against Russia's interests.
    • Angela Merkel's opposition to further sanctions against Russia in 2017 is an example of this[9].
    • Economic sanctions did hit Russia after the annexation.
    • While they were harmful, the EU still remained dependent on Russian exports[10].

    Part 4: Revolution & Crimea

    Russia's influence in Ukraine would be hampered in two revolutions.

    • Orange Revolution occured in 2004.
    • Led to ousting of Pro-Russian Government[11].
    • Important event, but it will not be focused on.
    • Revolution of Dignity occurred in early 2014.
    • Led to ousting of another Pro-Russian government.
    • Was the tipping point that led to the anexation of Crimea.
    • Russia invaded to secure the Sevastapol Port and to weaken Ukraine[12].
    • Russia would also support separatists in the Donetsk and Luhansk Regions.

    With a weakened Ukraine, Russia hoped to rexert its influence over the region and prevent Ukraine's entry into NATO, which would be a massive strategic loss for Russia.

    Why 2014 and not 2004?

    • Russia was in a much weaker economic state.
    • Most recent conflicts were two disasterous invasions of Chechnya.
    • Russia was still attempting to ally with the west.
    • 2014 Russia was simply more powerful than 2004 Russia[13].
    • More time needed to determine the actual economic impacts.

    Part 5: Today

    The war has been a disaster overall, but its effects on the Russian economy are still difficult to determine.

    • Russian military spending did dramatically increase, at the cost of other sectors.
    • Military spending potentially helped the economy grow slightly in 2023[14]
    • The ruble continued to decline in value, despite currency controls.
    • ~800,000 people emigrated from Russia[15]
    • Conflicting accounts on casualty numbers.
    • Potentially 315,000 casualties[16]

    Confounding Variables

    Rationality

    • This is assuming the decision to invade Ukraine a second time was rational.
    • Potential ulterior motives, such as regime security.
    • Nationalism is absolutely another motivator.
    • Information asymmetry is something that all parties had to deal with.

    Policy Proposal

    • Increased investments into developing countries
    • The goal being to keep authoritarianism from rising.
    • Harsher penalties for aggression
    • Which would make the costs of warfare far higher, thereby making the decision much more difficult.
    • Diversified economic interdependence (Globalism?)
    • Creating the insentive to trade without creating total dependence.
